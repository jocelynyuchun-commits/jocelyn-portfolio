Top Nav & PLP Redesign

Redesigning the global navigation and product listing experience for a major e-commerce platform — improving wayfinding, reducing bounce, and creating a scalable system that works across categories.

Overview

The existing top navigation and product listing pages hadn't been updated in three years. Analytics showed high bounce rates on category pages, low engagement with the nav menu, and a growing gap between mobile and desktop conversion rates.

The goal was to rethink both the navigation architecture and the PLP layout as a connected system — making it easier for users to find what they're looking for and browse with confidence.

Hero image — Final PLP design overview

Research & Discovery

We started with a two-week discovery sprint: reviewing analytics, running heatmap analysis on the existing PLP, and conducting 8 moderated user interviews to understand browsing and filtering behavior.

Key findings shaped the rest of the project: users weren't discovering sub-categories, filters were underused on mobile, and the nav mega-menu felt overwhelming with too many links competing for attention.

Analytics dashboard — bounce rate by category
Heatmap — existing PLP interaction patterns

Key insight: 62% of users who landed on a PLP left without clicking a single product. The primary driver wasn't lack of interest — it was decision paralysis from too many unfiltered options.

Information Architecture

Before touching UI, we restructured the category taxonomy. The existing nav had 140+ links across 4 levels. Card sorting with 12 participants helped us consolidate to a flatter, more intuitive hierarchy.

The new IA reduced top-level categories from 12 to 7, introduced curated "shop by" entry points, and gave sub-categories more descriptive labels.

Sitemap — before vs. after category hierarchy

Navigation Design

The redesigned nav moved from a dense mega-menu to a cleaner, category-focused panel. Each top-level category opens a contextual flyout showing sub-categories with visual cues — reducing cognitive load while maintaining deep access.

PLP Exploration

We explored three directions for the product listing page, each testing a different hypothesis about how users browse: grid-first with minimal filtering, filter-forward with a persistent sidebar, and a hybrid approach with smart defaults.

Option A — Grid-first, minimal filters
Option B — Filter-forward, persistent sidebar
Option C — Hybrid with smart defaults

Decision: User testing favored Option C. Participants appreciated having pre-applied filters they could remove, rather than starting from a blank slate.

Visual Design — Desktop

The final PLP design uses a clean 4-column product grid with a collapsible filter sidebar. Key refinements included larger product imagery, visible color swatches, and a sticky filter bar for quick sorting without scrolling back to the top.

Visual Design — Mobile

On mobile, the PLP shifts to a 2-column grid with a bottom-sheet filter panel. Horizontal scrolling chips at the top surface the most-used filters, so users can refine results without opening the full filter drawer.

Product Grid & Filters

The filter system was designed to be contextual: different categories surface different filter types. Apparel shows size, color, and fit; home goods shows material, room, and price range. This reduced irrelevant filter options by 40%.

Results & Impact

The redesign launched as an A/B test across two product categories before a full rollout. Results after 6 weeks showed meaningful improvements across key metrics.

Beyond the numbers, the new navigation and PLP system established a scalable framework that the team continued to build on — adding seasonal merchandising slots, A/B testable grid layouts, and category-specific entry points.

This project was part of a broader e-commerce platform modernization. Each decision was grounded in data and validated through testing before launch.
